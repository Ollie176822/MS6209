Semantic Search in AI

Semantic search in AI and large language models (LLMs) diverges from keyword-based search by focusing on meaning and context rather than exact word matches. This approach enables a better understanding of user intent and delivers more relevant results (Ahluwalia et al., 2024; Ni et al., 2024). LLMs leverage vast datasets and transformer models to grasp the semantics of queries, allowing for more accurate and contextually appropriate search outcomes (Ni et al., 2024). Semantic search techniques, such as embedding models, can capture deep semantic connections in complex texts, outperforming traditional keyword-based methods (Alqarni, 2024). While keyword-based search engines struggle to understand term relationships and meanings, semantic search engines demonstrate improved precision and better handling of natural language queries (Roy et al., 2019). Hybrid approaches combining keyword matching, semantic embeddings, and LLM-generated structured queries show promise in enhancing search relevance and accuracy (Ahluwalia et al., 2024).

UI/UX Design Decisions

This section explains key interface decisions made to optimize usability and clarity for academic users:

  • AI Semantic Search styling: Uses a distinctive gradient background to visually separate it from standard search and signify innovation.
  • Citation style selector: Enables users to choose citation formats, aligning the assistant with academic expectations and reducing rework.
  • Feedback buttons: Offer an intuitive way for users to rate results, simulating the kind of feedback loop modern intelligent systems rely on.

Risks, Limitations & Considerations

While semantic AI improves search relevance, it introduces challenges including potential hallucination of facts, lack of transparency in how results are ranked, and the risk of reinforcing systemic bias in datasets. User over-reliance on AI-synthesized summaries could also reduce critical engagement with source materials. Ethical oversight and ongoing algorithm auditing are essential to maintain academic integrity and fairness.

There is also the risk that students accept AI summaries without verifying primary sources, especially when the interface mimics authoritativeness. Encouraging critical thinking and providing source transparency are vital to avoid misuse.
